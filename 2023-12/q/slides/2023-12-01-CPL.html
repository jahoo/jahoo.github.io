when does unpredictable not mean difficult?

testing a belief-update theory of processing cost

Jacob Louis Hoover

MIT CPL meeting

2023-12-01

Motivation

slides at jahoo.github.io/2023-12

During incremental processing, effort is not constant.

Generally, if input less consistent with what we already believe \leadsto more effort to process.

\textit{Yesterday, I had a lasagna for}

  • ‚Ä¶ \textit{dinner}

    very predictable (‚úÖ no update) \leadsto easy

  • ‚Ä¶ \textit{breakfast}

    less predictable (‚ö†Ô∏è update!) \leadsto less easy

Intuition: changing what you believe takes effort. Can estimate this update-cost with surprisal

\surp{w} \coloneqq -\log p(w\mid \mathrm{context})

BUT, sometimes, a word can be very surprising without resulting in a large change in beliefs.

  • ‚Ä¶ \textit{dimmer}

    highly unpredictable \leadsto extremely difficult? no huge belief update, probably just means ‚Äòdinner‚Äô!

Proposed explanation: processing cost is update-cost (KL-divergence) prior -> posterior.

surprisal in a joint model

\operatorname{surprisal} = -\log p(w) \phantom{ = -\log \textstyle\sum_z p(z)p(w \mid z) = -\log \mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{\operatorname{prior}} \operatorname{likelihood} }

Joint model: variable Z is the structure / intended message, which gives rise to observation W

Z \to W

with message z having \operatorname{likelihood}=p(w \mid z), and prior p(z)

\implies to get surprisal, marginalize joint over all possible z (the negative log marginal likelihood):

\operatorname{surprisal} = -\log p(w ) % \phantom{ = -\log \textstyle\sum_z p(z )p(w \mid z) = -\log \mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{\operatorname{prior}} \operatorname{likelihood} % }

An observation will have large surprisal if it is unlikely under hypotheses given weight in the prior.

  1. unexpected continuations :e.g. \textit{breakfast} (large belief update)

  2. also typos: e.g., \textit{dimmer} note: surprisal large but not infinite, even if it was certainly not intended

    ‚Ä¶ belief update is small, how so?

I‚Äôm eliding an extra variable for context \mathrm{c}, \operatorname{surprisal} = -\log p(w \mid \mathrm{c} ) % \phantom{ = -\log \textstyle\sum_z p(z \mid \mathrm{c} )p(w \mid z, \mathrm{c}) = -\log \mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{\operatorname{prior}} \operatorname{likelihood} % }

Central intuition: Typos are unpredictable without causing huge belief updates

  • expectation through noise model (inside listener‚Äôs head) Model of the comprehender:

    • knows observed word might not be intended (has noise/error model) prob of observing w given intended z

  • walk through interpretation with dimmer

    • noise model doesn‚Äôt predict infinite surprisal for typo, but still predicts it is high, compared ‚Äòbreakfast‚Äô which causes larger shift in beliefs

  • reveal breakdown

  • go back to intuition

Proposing ‚ÄúKL theory‚Äù

Hypothesis: cost(w) \approx {\colorKL \text{belief update (KL divergence)}}:

{\colorKL\KL{\operatorname{posterior}}{\operatorname{prior}}} = %\overbrace{-\log \mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{\operatorname{prior}} \operatorname{likelihood} }^% {\surp{w}} - {\colorR\underbrace{\mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{\operatorname{posterior}}[-\log \operatorname{likelihood}]}_{\operatorname{R}(w)}}

How to think of these terms?

In the general case of a nondeterministic relationship between intended meanings and observed words:

  • \surp{w} the quantity of information in w.

  • {\colorR R(w)} the quantity of information that is cancelled (doesn‚Äôt contribute to belief update)

    ‚Äúreconstruction information‚Äù

Large R \implies w is substantially less difficult than predicted by surprisal.

With a deterministic relationship between intended meanings and observed words (Levy 2005, 2008):

  • R = 0 always, and KL = surprisal.

On last point:

  • This is a ‚Äòpointwise‚Äô definition, so anywhere where the relationship is deterministic, ‚ÄúKL theory‚Äù doesn‚Äôt differ from surprisal theory. (That is, when the relation between intended meanings and ovservable words is a function, or equiv, when the likelihood is binary)

Finding examples where R is non-negligible == what I‚Äôm interested in!

Aside: Motivation in terms of ideas about algorithms

Hypothesis: cost(w) \approx {\colorKL \text{belief update } {\colorKL\KL{\operatorname{posterior}}{\operatorname{prior}}} \text{)}}:

Why belief update as processing cost? (and why this particular measure of belief update size?)

This precise quantity shows up in potential algorithmic models

  • sampling-based models of approximate inference (Hoover et al. 2023)

    • e.g. importance sampling from prior: \#\text{samples}_{\mathrm{IS}(p\leftarrow q)} \approx e^{\KL p q}

  • models of perceptual discrimination (Norris 2006, 2009)

    • Drift-diffusion / sequential probability ratio test, number of steps scales in this KL.

AFAIK there aren‚Äôt candidate algorithms linking processing cost directly to surprisal.

Preliminary results:

Observations:

On average, each typo took longer to process than either of the corresponding non-typos.

Similar to surprisal prediction, (or not-easily-corrected typo in KL)

Larger surprisal items barely slower than smaller! (but significant, at 0.05 level, paired t-test)

Breaking down results by answer:

Q: are the typos easier when they are interpreted as having the less surprising meaning?

YES.

Typos take shorter when interpreted as the more expected meaning. (significant, paired t-test, at 0.05 level)

Breaking down by answer to the comrenension question: do the typos take shorter when they are interpreted as being the less surprising meaning? YES.

select only items where a majority of answers were ‚Äòas expected‚Äô in both of the non typo conditions (that is, implied_target_type == target_type)

Takeaways

Recall intuition:

despite being unpredictable (some) typos are not harder than (some) unexpected words.

Either not correct, or these first pilot stimuli didn‚Äôt get at this effectively.

If we want to capture this, need to design better stimuli that

  1. typo (‚ùì) is more natural (use naturally occuring typos? or, transposition of characters at least)

  2. unexpected word (‚ö†Ô∏è) is much more extremely unexpected

    not necessarily similar to expected word (remove confound, at expense of symmetry)

  3. use longer words overall, where reading times are less likely to be at floor

  • suggestions welcome!

KL theory derivation

\begin{aligned} \operatorname{KL}(p_{Z\mid w}\|p_{Z}) && = && \mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{p_{Z\mid w}}[\log \frac{p(z\mid w)}{p(z)}] && = && \mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{p_{Z\mid w}}[\log \frac{p(w\mid z)}{p(w)}]\\ && = && -\log p(w) && - && \mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{p_{Z\mid w}}[-\log p(w\mid z)]\\ && = && -\log \mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{p_{Z}}[p(w\mid z)] && - && \mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{p_{Z\mid w}}[-\log p(w\mid z)]\\ \operatorname{KL}(\operatorname{posterior}\|\operatorname{prior}) && = && \underbrace{-\log \mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{\operatorname{prior}} \operatorname{lik} }_{\operatorname{surprisal}} && - && \underbrace{\mathop{\mathbb{E}}_{\operatorname{posterior}}[-\log \operatorname{lik}]}_{\operatorname{R}} \end{aligned}
